## Q086 Design Doc: Behavioral Benchmark Suite for Listen vs Guess

**Goal:** Define 5 controlled evaluation environments where gc(k) measurement is maximally diagnostic — i.e., where a model that genuinely listens and a model that defaults to text priors produce *discriminably different* gc(k) profiles.

**Relation to Q098:** Q098 established the *motive-attribution framing* (why environments expose listening motive). Q086 is the *benchmark engineering spec* (how to construct, what to measure, success criteria).

---

## Design Constraints for a Valid gc(k) Benchmark Environment

1. **Text-audio contrast exists**: the audio contains information that conflicts with, extends, or cannot be recovered from the text context alone. Otherwise gc(k) cannot distinguish strategies.
2. **gc(k) prediction is falsifiable**: for each environment, we must specify in advance what gc(k) profile a "genuine listener" would produce vs a "prior-defaulter."
3. **No confounds with acoustic clarity alone**: some environments degrade SNR — we need to separately measure "can't hear" vs "won't use audio."
4. **CPU-feasible with mock data**: each ENV must be simulatable with toy data / MicroGPT before requiring Whisper/Qwen2-Audio. (Tier 0/1 implementation.)
5. **Connects to Paper A §2 claim**: "gc(k) predicts model listening behavior better than accuracy alone."

---

## The 5 Benchmark Environments

### ENV-1: Clean Speech (Baseline Listener)
**Purpose:** Establish gc(k) baseline for a fully attentive model on unambiguous audio.

**Construction:**
- Input: Clear speech at SNR ≥ 30 dB, phonemically unambiguous, no strong text prior
- Text context: Minimal (e.g., "Transcribe: [AUDIO]")
- Task: Phoneme-level transcription accuracy + gc(k) curve measurement

**gc(k) Prediction:**
- Genuine listener: High gc(k) peak at encoder's final layer (k* = Nenc). Peak α > 0.8. Sharp peak (AND-gate topology expected).
- Prior-defaulter: Low uniform gc(k) across layers. α < 0.3.

**Measurement:**
- Primary: Mean gc(k) across 100 samples; peak layer k*, peak amplitude α
- Secondary: AND-gate fraction at k* (% of features with AND-gate behavior)
- DoD signal: α ≥ 0.8, k* = final encoder layer, AND-gate fraction ≥ 60%

**Paper A role:** §3.1 (Methods: baseline characterization). Establishes "what good listening looks like mechanistically."

---

### ENV-2: Acoustic Degradation Gradient (When Can't → Won't)
**Purpose:** Distinguish "model can't hear" (low SNR) from "model won't listen" (prior preference). Tests gc(k) robustness as a motive detector across signal quality.

**Construction:**
- Input: Same phoneme (/p/ vs /b/) at 5 SNR levels: 40dB, 25dB, 15dB, 10dB, 5dB
- Text context: Strong prior predicting ONE of the two phonemes (unbalanced n-gram context)
- Task: Transcription + gc(k) at each SNR level

**gc(k) Prediction (KEY CONTRAST):**
- A model that "can't hear" at low SNR: gc(k) DROPS at low SNR, peak shifts earlier, AND-gate fraction decreases (signal-to-noise driven, not motive-driven). This is *expected* degradation.
- A model that "won't listen" (prior preference): gc(k) DROPS at HIGH SNR when text context is strong, even though audio is clear. This is the motive failure signature.
- Diagnostic: If gc(k) drop precedes SNR degradation (i.e., gc drops when audio is still clear but text prior is strong), that's motive-driven listening suppression.

**Measurement:**
- gc(k) vs SNR curve (5 points)
- "Listening persistence threshold": lowest SNR at which gc(k) peak remains ≥ 0.5
- Contrast: clean-audio + strong-text-prior trial vs noisy-audio + no-text-prior trial

**Paper A role:** §3.2 (Methods: motive vs capability dissociation). Critical for the claim "gc(k) detects listening motive, not just audio quality."

---

### ENV-3: Phoneme Conflict (Text Prior vs Audio Reality)
**Purpose:** Direct conflict between what the text predicts and what the audio says. The "hard test" for listening motive.

**Construction:**
- Input: Audio says /p/ (e.g., "pack"), text context strongly predicts /b/ (e.g., "carry a heavy ___")
- Exact conflict setup: audio = clear phoneme X, text N-gram probability of Y >> X
- Variants: (a) same-word conflict (/bad/ vs /pad/), (b) cross-word conflict (text predicts different word entirely)

**gc(k) Prediction:**
- Genuine listener: High gc(k) peak at encoder output; AND-gate features at k* activate. Model correctly transcribes X despite text predicting Y.
- Prior-defaulter: gc(k) drops (especially at decoder layers); model transcribes Y (text-predicted). Error correlates with gc(k) drop.
- **The falsifiable claim**: gc(k) at k* positively correlates with transcription accuracy on conflict trials (r > 0.6). Model is right when it listens.

**Measurement:**
- Per-trial: gc(k), predicted phoneme, correct phoneme
- Accuracy-gc(k) correlation on conflict trials
- AND-gate fraction on correct vs incorrect trials

**Paper A role:** §3.3 (Methods: conflict trials). Core empirical result if pattern holds. This is the most publishable ENV.

---

### ENV-4: Acoustic Information Trap (Heard but Not Said)
**Purpose:** Test vigilance — can the model detect absence of audio information that text context would fill in?

**Construction:**
- Audio: "I want a [SILENCE / static] pizza" (critical word replaced by silence or noise burst)
- Text context: "customer ordering delivery, wants a cheese pizza" (rich prior filling in "cheese")
- Task: What did the customer say? (model should report uncertainty or [INAUDIBLE], not hallucinate "cheese")

**gc(k) Prediction:**
- Genuine listener: gc(k) remains elevated until the silence position t_silence; then shows step-change drop (model detects gap). Collapse onset t* ≥ position of silence.
- Prior-defaulter: t* occurs early (model commits to "cheese" before reaching silence). gc(k) never reaches the silence position.
- This directly uses Q085 (collapse onset t*) as the measurement.

**Measurement:**
- t* (collapse onset step from Q085 analysis)
- Whether model output contains the hallucinated word vs [INAUDIBLE]/uncertainty
- Correlation: t* > t_silence → correct (did not hallucinate); t* < t_silence → hallucinated

**Paper A role:** §3.4 (Methods: vigilance test). Connects gc(k) to hallucination literature. Practical safety relevance.

---

### ENV-5: Rare Accent / Code-Switching (Updating Against Strong Prior)
**Purpose:** Test whether the model can update on rare-accent or code-switched audio even when text priors strongly predict the common pronunciation. The "audio wins" scenario.

**Construction:**
- Audio: Rare accent phoneme or code-switched word (e.g., Taiwanese Mandarin accent, Malaysian English)
- Text context: Predicts common (standard) pronunciation
- Correct answer: Rare-accent / code-switched form (audio wins)
- Samples: 50 trials where audio provides the MORE correct answer than text prior

**gc(k) Prediction:**
- Genuine listener: gc(k) peak HIGH specifically on "audio wins" trials. AND-gate fraction elevated on trials where model correctly uses audio over text.
- Prior-defaulter: gc(k) uniformly low; model outputs standard form regardless of audio.
- **Prediction (Q096 bridge)**: AND-gate fraction inversely correlates with text-predictability of the correct phoneme. High AND-gates = model "chose" to use audio over text.

**Measurement:**
- gc(k) on "audio wins" trials vs "text wins" trials (controls)
- AND-gate fraction per trial type
- Accuracy gap: rare-accent accuracy with vs without instruction to "listen carefully"

**Paper A role:** §3.5 (Methods: updating test) + Discussion (fairness implications — rare-accent speakers get more prior-dominated transcription if gc(k) is low).
