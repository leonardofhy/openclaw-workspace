## Problem

FAD (Fréchet Audio Distance) is the standard eval metric for text-to-audio generation, but scores diverge from human judgment. Why? The encoder's **training task** dictates which acoustic features are preserved or discarded — FAD inherits these biases.

## Method

Decompose FAD evaluation into 4 axes:
1. **Recall** — tolerance to intra-class stylistic variation
2. **Precision** — sensitivity to perceptual artifacts (noise, bandwidth loss, reverb)
3. **Semantic Alignment** — spectral identity, timbre, sound character
4. **Structural Alignment** — temporal ordering, event sequencing

6 encoders tested: AudioMAE, Whisper, VGGish, EnCodec, CLAP, + others.
Log-scale normalization to compare across encoders with vastly different dynamic ranges.

## Results (Key for gc(k) connection)

| Encoder | Training Task | Precision (artifacts) | Structural | Semantic | Recall |
|---------|--------------|----------------------|------------|---------|--------|
| AudioMAE | Reconstruction | **BEST** | Mid | Mid | Mid |
| **Whisper** | **ASR** | **BLIND** | **BEST** | Low | Low |
| VGGish | Classification | Mid | Low | **BEST** | Low (penalizes variation) |

**Whisper is structurally-dominant but signal-degradation-blind.** Its ASR training abstracts away pitch, timbre, and amplitude artifacts. It preserves *what was said* (temporal structure) at the cost of *how it sounds*.

**Formalism:** If encoder Φ is invariant to perturbation τ (i.e., Φ(x̃) ≈ Φ(x)), then FAD → 0 regardless of perceptual severity. This is the **invariance set** — Whisper's invariance set includes nearly all signal degradations.

## Connection to gc(k) Hypothesis ← KEY

The **FAD invariance set** concept is the mathematical backbone of what gc(k) is trying to measure:

- gc(k) = "at layer k, how much does the representation still encode raw audio signal vs. converged to text-prediction?"
- Whisper's training task creates an **embedding space bias** toward ASR-relevant structure (temporal ordering of phonemes) at the expense of acoustic fidelity
- This bias is not a bug: it's the optimized solution for ASR — the model **learns to ignore** signal-irrelevant acoustic variation
- In "Listen vs. Guess" language: Whisper layers with low gc(k) are **guessing** from text-prediction priors rather than **listening** to acoustic signal

**Causal claim this paper supports:**
- Whisper's late layers have LOW gc(k) because ASR training explicitly rewards prediction from linguistic context, not acoustic grounding
- The "structural alignment" dominance = Whisper tracks *what was said* but not *how it sounded*
- FAD computed with Whisper would score equally well a clean recording and a heavily noise-corrupted version (if temporal structure preserved)

## Open Questions

1. Can we directly map the FAD invariance set to the gc(k) profile layer-by-layer? The invariance set is measured at the final embedding layer; gc(k) sweeps all layers. The hypothesis: layers with high gc(k) are in the "precision-sensitive" regime; layers with low gc(k) are in the "structural/invariance" regime.

2. Whisper's final embedding is in the invariance set for signal degradation — but is the **Listen Layer** (high gc(k) mid-layers) NOT in this invariance set? This would be the smoking gun: Listen Layer = Whisper's last acoustic-sensitive layer before text-prediction takes over.

3. Paper argues for "evaluation-native encoders" aligned with human perception. Does gc(k) inform encoder design for better evaluation? Could a gc(k)-maximized encoder yield more perceptually-aligned FAD?
