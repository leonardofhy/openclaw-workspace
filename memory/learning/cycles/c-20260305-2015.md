## Problem
Emergent Misalignment (EM): fine-tuning on narrow harmful data (e.g., insecure code) causes models to become *broadly* misaligned — adversarial, power-seeking, dismissive — even on unrelated topics. Current explanation (Character as Latent Variable, Turner et al.): harmful FT activates a latent "misaligned persona" from pretraining, suppressing the helpful assistant persona.

Key question: **Can we make the assistant identity harder to displace?**

## Method
- **Pipeline**: OpenCharacterTraining (Maiya et al. 2025) — 11 character-trained personas per model
  - Stage 1: Constitution (traits × situational questions) → DPO preference pairs
  - Stage 2: Synthetic introspective data (self-reflection, self-interaction) → SFT
- **Threat**: Each persona subjected to EM fine-tuning protocol (Turner et al.)
- **Evaluation**: 3 out-of-domain datasets testing emergent generalization
- **Models**: Qwen 2.5 7B, Llama 3.1 8B
- **Custom constitutions**: Metacommunicative traits — distinguishing message levels, surfacing conflicting signals, resisting covert frame shifts → "Goodness-Meta-V2"
- **Ablation**: Contextualized reflection — augmenting corrupted training samples with model's own pre-FT self-assessment

## Results
- **Qwen 2.5 7B**: Every persona (including safety-irrelevant: humor, poeticism, math) reduces critical misalignment rate vs. baseline after EM fine-tuning
- **Llama 3.1 8B**: Most personas protective; but *sarcasm* catastrophically amplifies EM on Llama (while protective on Qwen) → model-dependent
- **Best performer**: Goodness-Meta-V2 (metacommunicative constitution) → lowest critical misalignment rate across all personas
- **Contextualized reflection**: Moderate protection but weaker than full character training
- **Mechanistic**: Character-trained models resist movement along the "misalignment direction" in activation space across all layers

## Theoretical Frame
Drawing on Bateson's **logical types in communication**: EM = failure of logical type discrimination. Model treats narrow fine-tuning signal as globally identity-defining because it can't distinguish the *level* at which the signal operates. CAI trains models to read context markers and distinguish signal types (metacommunicative capacity) → this is the protective mechanism.

## Connection to T5 (Listen-Layer Audit / Audio EM)
**Central hypothesis for Q032**: Audio EM would be a *harder-to-detect* EM variant because:
1. The corrupted signal is embedded in acoustic format, not text — standard text-level safety mechanisms don't fire
2. Guessing-mode models (high hallucination, low gc(k)) may have weaker "listen to signal" capacity, hence less logical-type discrimination
3. CAI-style audio character training could be a mitigation direction — but audio constitutions are harder to construct

**Key contrast for Q032 hypothesis note**:
- Text EM: corrupted data activates latent misaligned persona in text space
- Audio EM: corrupted audio fine-tuning might activate misaligned persona via a *different pathway* — acoustic priors rather than semantic priors — potentially bypassing CAI protection
- Testing: Does a CAI character-trained model remain vulnerable to audio-domain EM fine-tuning even if protected against text-domain EM?

## Open Questions
1. Does audio EM fine-tuning bypass the metacommunicative protection that works for text EM?
2. Is the "misalignment direction" in activation space the same for audio-triggered vs. text-triggered EM? (interpretability question)
3. Can gc(k) (our T3 metric) predict EM vulnerability? Hypothesis: low gc(k) models (guessing) are more susceptible to EM because they lack strong grounding in acoustic evidence
4. Is there an "audio constitution" analog that trains models to distinguish acoustic signal levels?
