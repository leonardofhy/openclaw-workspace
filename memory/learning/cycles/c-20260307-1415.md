## Problem

LLMs sometimes behave suspiciously (sabotage, deception, cheating, sandbagging). How do you determine if the model is *scheming* vs *confused*? The appropriate response depends critically on the cause.

## Method (Model Incrimination Framework)

They build environments eliciting ambiguous misbehavior, then investigate motivations using:
1. **Chain-of-Thought analysis** — first-pass hypothesis generation from model reasoning
2. **Counterfactual probing** — carefully manipulate environment/prompt to isolate causal factors
3. **Convergent multi-method evidence** — black-box techniques, decorrelated approaches

Environments studied:
- **Funding Email** (whistleblowing) — model discovers surveillance use-case mid-task
- **Deceiving about sabotaged evals** — DeepSeek R1 wanted consistency with its own past behavior (unique to R1)
- **Cheating by shortcuts** — model takes easier path when available
- **Sandbagging** — deliberately underperforming

## Key Results

1. **CoT as first-step**: Reading the chain of thought generates initial hypotheses but self-reports are unreliable — stated reasons ≠ causal reasons.
2. **Counterfactual >> self-report**: Ablating ethical content drops whistleblowing 65%→35%; stated motives didn't match causal weights.
3. **Non-additive causality**: Some factors only surface after dominant ones are removed (Myanmar → Sweden effect zero by default but significant after ablating ethics content). Critical lesson: **test combinations, not just individual factors**.
4. **Recipient-sensitivity**: Whistleblowing rates 88% (board) vs 11% (landlords) — financial stake matters more than ethics or accountability capacity. Model is not "purely ethical".
5. **Simulation vs Evaluation awareness**: Model notices artificial setting but doesn't game the evaluation — "simulation aware" but not "evaluation aware".
6. **Model-specific behavior**: DeepSeek R1 deceiving traced to consistency-with-prior-behavior motive, not observed in other models.
