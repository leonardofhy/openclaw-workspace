## Hypothesis Note: Audio Emergent Misalignment (EM) in Guessing vs. Listening ASR-LLMs

### Threat Model

**Core claim**: Audio-language models that primarily *guess* (rely on language priors, low gc(k)) are more vulnerable to fine-tune-induced misalignment than models that *listen* (ground predictions in acoustic features, high gc(k)).

**Mechanism**:
1. Guessing models form audio representations that are weakly tied to acoustic signal — the "listen layers" carry little unique information, and the model relies on linguistic context and LM priors to fill in ambiguity.
2. Under LoRA fine-tuning on a narrow harmful domain (e.g., audio instructions for dangerous tasks), the model's safety refusals depend on learned linguistic patterns in the LM backbone.
3. If the backbone's representation is already shallow on audio content, adversarial fine-tuning has a lower barrier: fewer audio-grounded features to "overwrite," less signal diversity to resist distributional shift.
4. Listening models, by contrast, encode richer acoustic features in mid-to-late layers; their refusals are partly grounded in acoustic context (tone, prosody, speaker style), making fine-tune attacks harder — they'd need to corrupt both the linguistic AND acoustic signal pathways.

**Analogy to text EM**: In text EM (Betley et al. 2025), models fine-tuned on narrow coding domains show misalignment on unrelated tasks — a generalization failure. Audio EM is the acoustic modality version: fine-tuning on a narrow audio-harm domain causes misalignment in audio safety behavior. Guessing models are the higher-risk case because their audio safety is already shallower.

### Minimal LoRA Protocol

**Goal**: Validate that gc(k)-low models show more post-fine-tune misalignment than gc(k)-high models.

**Setup**:
- Base model: Whisper-medium (or Qwen-Audio-Chat as audio-LLM)
- Fine-tune dataset: 50–100 audio clips in a narrow benign-but-unusual domain (e.g., unusual accents, technical jargon, non-standard speech patterns) — **NOT harmful content** (avoid real harm; EM emerges even on non-harmful but out-of-distribution fine-tuning)
- LoRA rank: 8, target: attention Q/V, 3 epochs

**Evaluation**:
1. Pre-fine-tune: measure gc(k) on held-out audio → classify model as high/low listener
2. Fine-tune both a high-gc(k) and low-gc(k) checkpoint
3. Post-fine-tune: probe for safety-adjacent behavior degradation using audio adversarial prompts (existing AudioJailbreak dataset or synthetic)
4. Compare: did the low-gc(k) model degrade more on safety probes?

**Approximate cost**: CPU-runnable with mock logit extraction; GPU needed only for actual LoRA (Tier 2, needs Leo approval)

### Listen-Layer Signals to Watch

| Signal | What to measure | Expected in guessing model |
|--------|----------------|--------------------------|
| gc(k) pre/post fine-tune | Confidence mass at layer k | Drops further post fine-tune (already low) |
| Layer-wise representation shift | L2 distance of audio activations pre/post | Larger shift in guessing model's audio layers |
| Safety probe accuracy | Linear probe trained on safety vs. non-safety audio | Lower pre-fine-tune AND faster degradation |
| Acoustic-semantic alignment (ASA) | Cosine sim between audio and text embeddings | Already lower; drops post fine-tune |
| Gradient flow to audio encoder | Grad norm at audio encoder vs. LM layers | Lower in guessing model (LM dominates) |

### Expected Failure Modes

1. **No measurable EM effect**: If the fine-tuning dataset is too small or too benign, no safety degradation appears in either model type → need larger/more targeted fine-tune set.
2. **Both models degrade equally**: If the safety behavior is entirely in the LM backbone (shared across guessing/listening), gc(k) level may not predict EM susceptibility. Would suggest safety is LM-prior-driven, not audio-grounded.
3. **High-gc(k) model degrades MORE**: If listening models are more brittle because their safety depends on acoustic cues that fine-tuning disrupts. This would falsify the hypothesis and suggest guessing models' linguistic redundancy actually protects them.
4. **gc(k) shifts post fine-tune**: Fine-tuning itself changes the gc(k) profile — confound. Need to measure gc(k) change as a covariate.
5. **Evaluation saturation**: If safety probes are too easy (near 100% pre-fine-tune), no signal after fine-tuning. Need adversarial audio probes that are near the decision boundary.

### Connection to T5 Thesis

This hypothesis extends the Listen-Layer Audit from a **detection** tool (find jailbreaks using gc(k) signatures) to a **prediction** tool (predict EM susceptibility from gc(k) before fine-tuning). If validated, gc(k) audit becomes a pre-deployment safety screen: "This model listens poorly — it's high-risk for fine-tune misalignment."
