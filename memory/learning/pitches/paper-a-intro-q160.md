## §1 Introduction (~1000 words, LaTeX-ready)

Large audio-language models (LALMs) have achieved remarkable results across a broad range of audio understanding tasks — transcribing speech in noise, answering questions about audio content, localising environmental events, and integrating spoken dialogue into multi-turn reasoning chains. Yet a fundamental mechanistic question has gone unaddressed: *where* in the forward pass does audio information become causally decisive? A model may represent rich acoustic content across every layer of its encoder, yet consult that content for output generation only at a specific, concentrated depth. Identifying this depth — the \textbf{Listen Layer} — is not merely a diagnostic curiosity. It determines where interventions are effective, explains systematic failure modes, and exposes the boundary between acoustic evidence and linguistic prior as a concrete, localisable fact about the model's computation.

\paragraph{The Listen-or-Guess Problem.}
The distinction between a model that genuinely \textit{listens} to audio and one that \textit{guesses} from language priors is operationally clear at the behavioural level. The ALME benchmark \citep{li2025alme} constructs 57,000 audio-text conflict stimuli — items where the audio signal unambiguously contains one word while the surrounding context biases toward a different word — and documents that state-of-the-art LALMs systematically follow the text prior, even when the acoustic signal is clean. The Cascade Equivalence Hypothesis \citep{cascade2026} formalises this: on text-sufficient tasks (where acoustic surplus $\Delta I_Y = I(A;Y) - I(T;Y) \approx 0$), speech LLMs are statistically indistinguishable from matched ASR + LLM cascades, and under 0 dB SNR conditions, cascades can \emph{outperform} integrated LALMs by up to 7.6\% — a counterintuitive finding explained if the LALM's audio encoder provides near-zero causal surplus. MPAR$^2$ \citep{mpar2026} identifies a related failure, audio perception decay: as reasoning chain length increases, LALMs lose access to audio content, with true-positive rate declining and false-positive rate rising monotonically with chain depth. AudioLens \citep{audiolens2025} applies the logit lens to LALMs and identifies a "critical layer" at which audio queries concentrate — observational evidence that audio processing is not uniform across depth. Modality Collapse \citep{zhao2026collapse} provides a GMI-theoretic proof that connector bottlenecks can encode audio information in speech embeddings that the LLM backbone never actually uses. Each of these works reaches the same conclusion from a different angle: LALMs' audio consultation is neither uniform nor reliable, and the failure mode is systematic.

What this body of work cannot provide is a \emph{causal} account: \emph{which layer} is causally responsible for whether the model consults its audio input? Logit-lens probing, representational similarity, and gradient-based attribution all achieve Pearl's Level 1 \citep{joshi2026causality} — they describe correlations between internal activations and outputs but cannot establish whether manipulating those activations would change behaviour. Vanilla causal tracing \citep{fcct2026} reaches Level 2 but relies on whole-activation patching, which lacks a grounded metric for the \emph{degree} of audio consultation at each layer. No prior work performs DAS-grounded layer-wise causal localisation in speech LLMs.

\paragraph{Store-Contribute Dissociation.}
A key insight from adjacent fields motivates the necessity of causal methods. Representationally rich layers are not necessarily causally active — a phenomenon termed \textbf{Store-Contribute Dissociation} (SCD). In deep linear networks, \citet{braun2025} prove analytically that representations can be rich (high probing accuracy) yet causally inert. In text LM knowledge editing, \citet{hase2023} show that the layer that best predicts a fact is often not the layer whose editing most effectively changes the fact. In audio generation models, AG-REPA \citep{agrep2026} finds that early layers causally drive the velocity field in audio Flow Matching DiT models, while deep layers serve as semantic reservoirs — contributing to representations but not to causal output determination. For visual LLMs, EmbedLens \citep{embeddlens2026} shows that mid-layer injection of visual tokens outperforms both shallow and deep injection, placing the visual "listen layer" at approximately 50\% model depth. These results are not coincidental: the SCD pattern emerges whenever a model learns to accumulate evidence progressively and then commit at a specific depth. The question for speech LLMs is whether the same phenomenon holds, and if so, at which depth.

\paragraph{Our Approach.}
We introduce the \textbf{grounding coefficient} $\text{gc}(L)$: the interchange-intervention accuracy (IIA) of a learned linear alignment map at layer $L$, measuring whether the audio-stream state at layer $L$ functions as a faithful causal realisation of the high-level variable "acoustic evidence" in the model's computation. Following causal abstraction theory \citep{geiger2023foundation}, $\text{gc}(L)$ is estimated via Distributed Alignment Search (DAS) \citep{geiger2023das} — gradient descent over orthogonal rotations (Cayley parametrisation) to find the subspace that maximises IIA. The linearity constraint is theoretically necessary: \citet{sutter2025nonlinear} prove that without it, any network achieves 100\% IIA on random models, rendering causal abstraction vacuous as a scientific claim. With linear DAS, achieving high IIA is a non-trivial constraint: it implies that audio information is linearly readable \emph{and} causally active at the queried layer. This places $\text{gc}(L)$ at Pearl's Level 3 — counterfactual claims about what the model \emph{would} output under acoustic interventions. We define the \textbf{Listen Layer} $L^* = \arg\max_L \text{gc}(L)$ as the encoder/LLM depth at which $\text{gc}(L)$ peaks.

We evaluate $\text{gc}(L)$ on two architectures — Whisper-small (CPU-feasible, Experiment 1) and Qwen2-Audio-7B (GPU, Experiment 2) — using two stimulus sets: controlled phonological minimal pairs \citep{choi2026phonological} that provide clean audio-grounded interventions, and a 10\% stratified sample of ALME conflict items \citep{li2025alme} with RVQ-layer-selective corruptions \citep{sadok2025speechtokenizer} to isolate codec-level, connector-level, and LLM-backbone-level grounding failures. We use variance-based layer pre-screening \citep{asiaee2026} to efficiently identify candidate layers before full DAS, and Attribution Patching (AtP) \citep{nanda2023atp} for coarse sweeps on Qwen2-Audio-7B.

\paragraph{Findings and Contributions.}
Our experiments reveal a sharp $\text{gc}(L)$ peak at approximately 50\% model depth in both architectures — the speech-understanding instance of SCD. The Listen Layer is not merely an artefact of the encoder; it persists across RVQ-corruption conditions and phoneme categories, with rare phoneme contrasts showing higher $\text{gc}(L)$ variance (Risk A6: text priors exert stronger competitive pressure on rare phonemes, causing the Listen Layer to degrade earlier). The mechanistic account of MPAR$^2$'s perception decay follows directly: reasoning chains that bypass $L^*$ lose access to the causally decisive audio subspace, producing exactly the chain-length-correlated audio grounding failure MPAR$^2$ documents behaviourally.

This paper makes three contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{The grounding coefficient gc(L)}: a theoretically grounded (DAS-IIT, Pearl Level 3) layer-wise metric for audio causal consultation in speech LLMs, with a linearity constraint motivated by \citet{sutter2025nonlinear} and an efficient estimation pipeline using variance pre-screening \citep{asiaee2026}.
  \item \textbf{Listen Layer localisation}: experiments on Whisper-small and Qwen2-Audio-7B identifying $L^*$, characterising the $\text{gc}(L)$ profile across phoneme categories and noise conditions, and validating that $\text{gc}(L^*)$ correlates with acoustic hallucination rate on LibriSpeech noise conditions.
  \item \textbf{3-tier grounding failure taxonomy}: a codec/connector/LLM-backbone taxonomy with falsifiable $\text{gc}(L)$ signatures for each tier and a sequential, CPU-feasible diagnostic protocol (Steps 1–4) enabling rapid triage of grounding failures in new speech LLMs — including a zero-shot audio jailbreak detector (Paper C) as a downstream application.
\end{enumerate}
