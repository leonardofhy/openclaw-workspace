### DAS Assumption Analysis (for Paper A)

**Assumption 1: The audio grounding variable is linearly encodable in the residual stream**
- DAS learns a *rotation* (linear change of basis) â†’ this only works if the causal variable is in a *linear subspace*
- Evidence FOR: Linear Representation Hypothesis (Mikolov, Park et al.) broadly confirmed in text; Choi et al. (2602.18899) confirms linear phonological geometry in S3M models â†’ audio representations ARE linear
- Evidence AGAINST: Connector bottleneck may create non-linear projections â†’ Gap #18 pre-flight tests this
- **Risk level:** MEDIUM. Gap #18 experiment (Priority 0) doubles as linearity validation. If phonological geometry doesn't survive connector, DAS assumption is fragile for full LALM (Qwen2-Audio); Whisper-only claim is still valid.
- **Mitigation:** Phase 1 (Whisper-small) doesn't involve a connector â€” linearity assumption safer. Phase 2 (Qwen2-Audio) reports both vanilla patching AND DAS-IIA; discrepancy = a finding, not a failure.

**Assumption 2: The "listen vs guess" variable is binary (clean audio-grounded vs text-predicted)**
- DAS needs a well-defined high-level causal model. Binary is clean. But grounding may be graded.
- **Risk level:** LOW. The ALME 57K stimuli (Li et al.) are designed as binary conflict pairs â€” audio answer â‰  text answer. Binary causal model fits these stimuli directly.
- **Mitigation:** Report graded gc(k) = IIA as continuous (not binary threshold). DAS loss is already continuous.

**Assumption 3: DAS learns the RIGHT subspace (not a spurious correlate)**
- Gradient descent could find a subspace that satisfies the IIT objective without being the *semantically meaningful* grounding subspace
- **Risk level:** MEDIUM. Standard in DAS literature â€” addressed by cross-generalization: train DAS on clean/corrupt audio minimal pairs; test on NEW minimal pairs (different phonemes, different speakers). If IIA holds on held-out pairs â†’ subspace is generalizing, not overfitting.
- **Mitigation:** 80/20 train/test split on Choi et al. stimuli. Cross-generalization = Paper A Figure 3.

**Assumption 4: IIA peak layer = "where audio grounding happens" (causal claim)**
- IIA peak could reflect where the *probe* is easiest (high linear separability) rather than where grounding causally occurs
- **Risk level:** MEDIUM. This is the PROBE_LAYER â‰  INTERVENE_LAYER issue (already in Paper A v0.2 method). Resolved by sweeping both independently.
- **Mitigation:** Report gc(L_probe, L_intervene) as 2D heatmap. Primary claim = diagonal (L_probe = L_intervene). Supplements show off-diagonal effects.

**Assumption 5: gc(k) = DAS-IIA is a better metric than vanilla patching for "the" listen layer**
- DAS may find a DIFFERENT layer than vanilla patching. Is that the "real" listen layer, or an artifact?
- **Insight from cycle #101:** In the NLI case, DAS found that "entailment" is two word-identity representations â€” DAS revealed TRUE structure that localist patching MISSED. In Leo's case: DAS may reveal that "audio grounding" is distributed across layers (not a single sharp peak), while vanilla patching shows nothing because the subspace is distributed within each layer.
- **If DAS and vanilla disagree:** DAS wins (theoretically). Vanilla patching = localist assumption = wrong for polysemantic neurons.
- **Risk level:** LOW. Disagreement is a FINDING, not a failure. "We show DAS reveals a listen layer that vanilla patching misses" = stronger paper.
