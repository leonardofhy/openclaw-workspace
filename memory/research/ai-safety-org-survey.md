# Global survey of AI Safety organizations

**The AI safety ecosystem has grown from a handful of pioneer organizations to a sprawling global network of 200+ entities spanning academia, industry, government, nonprofits, and grassroots communities.** This transformation accelerated dramatically after 2023, when governments established national AI Safety Institutes, major companies published frontier safety frameworks, and philanthropic funding crossed an estimated $100M annually. The field remains heavily concentrated in the San Francisco Bay Area and London/Oxford corridor, but East Asian institutions — particularly in China, Japan, and South Korea — are rapidly building parallel infrastructure. What follows is the most comprehensive catalog available of organizations working on AI safety, alignment, governance, ethics, and responsible AI development.

## 2. Corporate AI Safety teams and companies

The corporate AI safety landscape ranges from companies built entirely around safety (Anthropic, SSI) to dedicated teams within tech giants, to a growing startup ecosystem building safety evaluation, red-teaming, and security tools.

### Safety-first AI labs

**Anthropic** (San Francisco) is the most prominent safety-focused AI lab, scoring highest (C+) on FLI's AI Safety Index. Founded by former OpenAI researchers Dario and Daniela Amodei, it publishes a Responsible Scaling Policy and leads in alignment research and interpretability. Jan Leike, former OpenAI Superalignment co-lead, joined in 2024. Website: anthropic.com

**Safe Superintelligence Inc. (SSI)** (Palo Alto & Tel Aviv) was founded in 2024 by Ilya Sutskever after leaving OpenAI. Its sole focus is building safe superintelligence. Raised **$1B seed round** in 2024, reached a $30B valuation by March 2025. ~20 employees, no products yet. Website: ssi.inc

### Major tech company safety teams

**OpenAI** (San Francisco) has experienced significant safety team instability. The **Superalignment team** was disbanded in May 2024 after both co-leads departed. The **AGI Readiness team** was disbanded in October 2024. The **Mission Alignment team** was disbanded in February 2026. Safety is now decentralized across product and research teams. The **Preparedness team** handles pre-deployment risk analysis. OpenAI launched a Safety Evaluations Hub in May 2025. Website: openai.com

**Google DeepMind** (London & Mountain View) maintains the Frontier Safety Framework with Critical Capability Levels covering misuse, ML R&D, and misalignment risks. Dedicated teams work on alignment, interpretability, and model evaluations. Google also operates a separate **Responsible AI team** focused on fairness, accountability, and transparency, plus an **AI Red Team** collaborating with Google's Secure AI Framework (SAIF). Website: deepmind.google

**Meta** (Menlo Park) publishes a frontier AI safety policy and releases open-source Llama models with Llama Guard for safety, but scored poorly (D range) on FLI's Safety Index. Created **Meta Superintelligence Labs** in 2025. Website: ai.meta.com

**Microsoft** (Redmond) has a Responsible AI division led by Sarah Bird, developing transparency, fairness, and safety tools. Major investor in OpenAI and Anthropic ($6.75B). Website: microsoft.com/en-us/ai/principles-and-approach

**NVIDIA** (Santa Clara) developed open-source safety tools including **NeMo Guardrails**, **NVIDIA Garak** (red-teaming scanner), and **NVIDIA Halos** (physical AI safety stack). Website: nvidia.com

**Amazon** (Seattle) provides AWS Bedrock Guardrails for enterprise safety, invested $6.75B in Anthropic. **Apple** (Cupertino) maintains internal ML safety efforts but is less publicly active. **IBM** (Armonk) launched watsonx.governance for ethical AI workflows. **Salesforce** (San Francisco) runs the Office of Ethical & Humane Use and developed the Einstein Trust Layer.

## 3. Nonprofit organizations

Nonprofits form the intellectual backbone of the AI safety field, conducting foundational technical research and field-building work that often predates government or corporate engagement.

### Technical research nonprofits

**Machine Intelligence Research Institute (MIRI)** (Berkeley) is one of the oldest AI safety organizations, founded in 2000. It focuses on foundational mathematical research on alignment and agent foundations. Website: intelligence.org

**Alignment Research Center (ARC)** (Berkeley) was founded by Paul Christiano and focuses on formal mechanistic explanations of neural network behavior. Website: alignment.org

**METR (Model Evaluation and Threat Research)** (Berkeley), formerly ARC Evals, evaluates frontier AI models for autonomous capabilities and dangerous risks. Works with OpenAI, Google DeepMind, and government AISIs on pre-deployment evaluations. Developed the "time horizon" metric. Website: metr.org

**Redwood Research** (Berkeley) conducts prosaic alignment research, known for work on alignment faking and AI control. Received ~$25M from Open Philanthropy. Website: redwoodresearch.org

**Center for AI Safety (CAIS)** (San Francisco) conducts technical safety research, offers a compute cluster to researchers, and organized the landmark 2023 statement on AI existential risk signed by **600+ AI leaders**. Founded by Dan Hendrycks. Website: safe.ai

**Apollo Research** (London) focuses on reducing risks from dangerous AI capabilities, especially scheming behaviors. Found strategic deception in OpenAI's o3 model. Website: apolloresearch.ai

**Palisade Research** (US) focuses on civilization-scale risks from agentic AI, finding frontier models resist shutdown. Research cited in congressional hearings. Website: palisaderesearch.org

**FAR AI** (Berkeley) conducts safety research, runs a coworking space, and makes grants to academics and independent researchers. Website: far.ai

**Epoch AI** (global) investigates key trends shaping AI trajectory — compute trends, training data, and capability forecasting. Website: epoch.ai

### Advocacy and field-building nonprofits

**Future of Life Institute (FLI)** (Cambridge, MA & Brussels) reduces catastrophic risks from AI through advocacy, grantmaking, and the AI Safety Index. Organized the influential 2023 open letter calling for an AI development pause. Website: futureoflife.org

**Partnership on AI (PAI)** (San Francisco) is a multi-stakeholder nonprofit including OpenAI, Google, Apple, Meta, Microsoft, and major NGOs setting shared responsible AI norms. Website: partnershiponai.org

**Center for Humane Technology** (San Francisco) focuses on technology supporting shared well-being and democracy. Created "The AI Dilemma" presentation. Website: humanetech.com

**PauseAI** (global) campaigns to convince governments to pause AI development through public outreach and protests. Website: pauseai.info

**The Midas Project** (US) is a watchdog nonprofit monitoring tech companies and raising awareness about safety corner-cutting. Website: themidasproject.com

**AI Safety Foundation** (Canada) is a registered charity founded by Geoffrey Hinton to increase awareness of AI's catastrophic risks. Runs annual Hinton Lectures. Website: aisfoundation.ai

**Concordia AI** (Beijing/global) is the key bridge organization for China-West AI safety dialogue. Publishes "State of AI Safety in China" reports and the AI Safety in China newsletter. Co-authored China's first frontier AI risk framework with Shanghai AI Lab. Website: concordia-ai.com

**AI Safety Asia (AISA)** (Singapore-based) harmonizes international AI safety governance across Asia. Conducted first AI safety training for Indonesian government officials. Website: aisafety.asia

### Additional nonprofits

Other notable organizations include the **Center for AI and Digital Policy (CAIDP)** (DC) assessing national AI policies globally; **Montreal AI Ethics Institute** (Montreal) for community-driven AI ethics research; **Centre for Long-Term Resilience (CLTR)** (London) for UK policy on AI risks; **Existential Risk Observatory** (Netherlands) for public awareness; **Center on Long-Term Risk (CLR)** (London) addressing worst-case suffering risks; **Cooperative AI Foundation** (UK) supporting cooperative intelligence research with ~$15M; **Evitable** (UK/Canada) founded by David Krueger for public organizing around AI risks; **Legal Safety Lab** (Europe) using legal advocacy to address frontier AI risks; and the **London Initiative for Safe AI (LISA)** providing workspace and operational support to AI safety organizations.

## 4. Government and intergovernmental bodies

Government engagement in AI safety underwent a step-change after the Bletchley Park Summit in November 2023, with national AI Safety Institutes proliferating worldwide and comprehensive legislation advancing in the EU and South Korea.

### United States

The **US AI Safety Institute (AISI)**, established within NIST in November 2023, was **renamed the Center for AI Standards and Innovation (CAISI)** in June 2025 under the Trump administration, shifting focus from safety regulation to pro-innovation standards. It houses the **AI Safety Institute Consortium (AISIC)** of 290+ member organizations and established the TRAINS Taskforce for national security AI testing. The Biden-era Executive Order on AI (Oct 2023) was revoked; the new administration emphasizes "pro-growth AI policies." The **FTC** launched Operation AI Comply targeting deceptive AI claims, though enforcement has been partially scaled back. California's **SB 1047** (frontier AI safety bill) was vetoed by Governor Newsom in September 2024, but transparency laws like AB 2013 were enacted.

### United Kingdom

The **UK AI Safety Institute** — renamed the **AI Security Institute** in February 2025 — was the world's first state-backed AI safety institute. With a budget of **~£50M/year** and 100+ technical staff, it has tested 16+ frontier models pre-release and released the open-source evaluation tool **Inspect**. It launched the **Alignment Project** (£27M+ in 60+ research grants across 8 countries) and published the first **International AI Safety Report** (96 experts, 30 countries). Website: aisi.gov.uk

### European Union

The **European AI Office** (Brussels), established February 2024 with 140+ staff, monitors, supervises, and enforces the **EU AI Act** — the world's first comprehensive AI law (adopted June 2024). The Act uses a risk-based framework with fines up to **7% of worldwide turnover** for prohibited practices. Implementation spans 2024–2027. Spain created Europe's first dedicated AI supervisory agency (**AESIA**) in December 2023. France partnered LNE and INRIA for AI evaluation and hosted the Paris AI Action Summit (Feb 2025). The **Council of Europe** adopted the first legally binding international AI treaty — the Framework Convention on AI — with **41 signatories**.

### Japan

The **Japan AI Safety Institute (J-AISI)** launched February 2024 within the Information-technology Promotion Agency, established by 10 ministries. ~25 technical staff focus on safety evaluation, red-teaming methodology, and international standards crosswalks. Japan's May 2025 act on AI R&D and utilization gives J-AISI a central legal role. Japan chaired the **G7 Hiroshima AI Process**, producing the first major multilateral framework for advanced AI governance. Website: aisi.go.jp

### South Korea

South Korea enacted the **AI Basic Act** (passed December 2024, effective January 2026) — the world's second comprehensive AI law. The **Korea AI Safety Institute (K-AISI)**, launched late 2024 within ETRI, runs a consortium of 24 entities. The **National AI Strategy Committee** under the President's Office serves as the central coordinating body. South Korea hosted the **AI Seoul Summit** (May 2024) where 16 leading AI companies signed Frontier AI Safety Commitments.

### China

China operates through multiple agencies: the **Cyberspace Administration of China (CAC)** is the primary AI regulator overseeing algorithm filing (1,400+ registered) and generative AI service registration (238 approved in 2024). The **National Information Security Standardization Technical Committee (TC260)** published the AI Safety Governance Framework (v1.0 Sept 2024, v2.0 Sept 2025). The **China AI Safety and Development Association (CnAISDA)**, launched February 2025 at the Paris Summit, serves as China's AISI equivalent — a decentralized coalition of leading institutions including Tsinghua, Peking University, BAAI, and Shanghai AI Lab. Key regulations include the Interim Measures for Generative AI Services (July 2023), among the world's first generative AI regulations.

### Taiwan and Singapore

Taiwan passed an **AI Basic Act** in December 2025 with seven core principles. The National Science and Technology Council is the designated central authority. **Singapore's AISI** (within IMDA) published the world's first AI testing toolkit (AI Verify, 2022) and governance frameworks for generative and agentic AI. Singapore co-leads international multilingual AI safety testing.

### International bodies

The **OECD AI Principles** (2019, revised 2024) were endorsed by 46 countries. The **Global Partnership on AI (GPAI)** merged with OECD's AI work in July 2024, now encompassing 44 member countries and 500+ experts. The **UN High-Level Advisory Body on AI** published "Governing AI for Humanity" (Sept 2024). **UNESCO's Recommendation on the Ethics of AI** (2021) applies to all 194 Member States. **ISO/IEC JTC 1/SC 42** published ISO/IEC 42001:2023, the AI Management System standard. The **International Network of AI Safety Institutes** (UK, US, Japan, France, Germany, Italy, Singapore, South Korea, Australia, Canada, EU) launched at the Seoul Summit, though its future faces uncertainty under the US administration's reorientation.

## 6. Community groups, fellowships, and grassroots organizations

The grassroots layer of AI safety is remarkably active, with research fellowships serving as the primary talent pipeline for the field. An estimated **70+ university AI safety groups** now exist worldwide.

### Research fellowships and training programs

**MATS (ML Alignment Theory Scholars)** (Berkeley & London) is the premier 9-week research fellowship, offering $15K stipends, $12K compute budgets, and mentorship from top researchers. **200+ alumni**, with 80% remaining in alignment work. Alumni founded Apollo Research, ARENA, and Timaeus. Website: matsprogram.org

**ARENA (Alignment Research Engineer Accelerator)** (London) runs a 5-week ML engineering bootcamp covering transformers, mechanistic interpretability, evals, and RL. Run 7+ times. Alumni proceed to MATS, Apollo, METR, and UK AISI.

**PIBBSS** (Prague & London) is a 3-month summer fellowship pairing PhDs from natural/social sciences with alignment mentors. Funded by Open Philanthropy. Website: pibbss.ai

**AI Safety Camp (AISC)** (online, global) runs 3-month part-time research programs (10 hrs/week) since 2018 with 10+ editions and ~27 projects per edition. Website: aisafety.camp

**BlueDot Impact** (London, global online) offers free courses in AI Alignment, Frontier AI Governance, and Technical AI Safety to **4,000+ professionals in 100+ countries**. Alumni work at Anthropic, OpenAI, and UK AISI. Website: bluedot.org

**Apart Research** (Copenhagen/London) runs monthly AI safety research hackathons and the Apart Lab 3–6 month fellowship. Publications at NeurIPS and ACL. Website: apartresearch.com

Other fellowships include **GovAI Summer/Winter Fellowships** (Oxford), **ERA (Existential Risk Alliance)** (UK), **Pivotal Research** (London), **Talos Network** (global), **LASR Labs** (London), **SPAR (Student Program for Alignment Research)**, and **Kairos programs** supporting university organizers.

### Coworking spaces and hubs

**LISA (London Initiative for Safe AI)** in Shoreditch, London, houses Apollo Research, BlueDot Impact, MATS London, ARENA, LASR Labs, PIBBSS, and independent researchers — making it the largest AI safety hub outside the Bay Area.

**Constellation** (Berkeley) hosts AI safety organizations and independent researchers. **FAR Labs** (Berkeley) provides research office space. **Lighthaven** (Berkeley) is an event space run by Lightcone Infrastructure. **CEEALAR** (Blackpool, UK) provides free food and housing for up to two years for people working on EA projects including AI safety.

### Online communities and platforms

**LessWrong** and the **Alignment Forum** (maintained by Lightcone Infrastructure) are the primary online venues for AI safety discussion and research. The **EA Forum** covers AI safety as a major topic area. **AISafety.com** provides a comprehensive field map. **AI Safety Support** offers courses, events, and 1-on-1 mentoring. **EleutherAI's Discord** hosts active safety discussions.

### Student groups

Notable university groups include **MAIA** (MIT), **AISST** (Harvard), **BAISSI** (UC Berkeley), **OxAI Safety Hub** (Oxford), **Safe AI London**, CMU AI Safety Club, Georgia Tech AI Safety, Cornell AI Safety, and groups at Northwestern, UBC, Delft, Groningen, University of Toronto, FGV (Brazil), and AI Safety Argentina.

### Career guidance

**80,000 Hours** (London/Oxford) identifies AI safety as their top priority problem and provides career reviews, curated job boards, podcasts, and free 1-on-1 advising. Website: 80000hours.org

**Coefficient Giving** (formerly Open Philanthropy) (San Francisco) is the single largest funder, backed by Dustin Moskovitz and Cari Tuna. **Survival and Flourishing Fund (SFF)** (Silicon Valley) is the second-largest funder, primarily backed by Jaan Tallinn. Donated **~$53M+ to AI safety since 2019, ~$34M in 2025 alone** (86% to AI causes). Runs annual S-Process grant rounds and rolling Speculation Grants. Website: survivalandflourishing.fund

**Navigation Fund** (Brooklyn, NY), founded by crypto billionaire Jed McCaleb, plans ~$20M/year for AI safety with total assets of ~$1.3B. Grantmaking reached $36.8M in 2024. Website: navigation.org

**Astralis Foundation** (Stockholm/London/NY/Vienna) gives **$25M annually**, backing AI governance structures, European AI safety leadership, and international dialogues. Website: astralisfoundation.org

**Long-Term Future Fund (LTFF)** (global, EA Funds) makes rolling grants to individuals and small projects. About half of funding historically came from Open Philanthropy. Website: funds.effectivealtruism.org

## Conclusion: a field at an inflection point

Three structural features define the AI safety landscape in early 2026. First, **extreme geographic concentration persists** — the Bay Area and London/Oxford corridor account for the vast majority of organizations, talent, and funding, though East Asia is building rapidly with dedicated AISIs in Japan, South Korea, and China's CnAISDA coalition.

Second, **institutional instability is a recurring theme**. Oxford's FHI closed after 19 years. OpenAI disbanded three dedicated safety teams in under two years. Both the US and UK renamed their AI Safety Institutes, signaling political reorientation. The field's institutional infrastructure remains younger and more fragile than its intellectual contributions suggest.

Third, the **funding ecosystem is dangerously concentrated**. Coefficient Giving (formerly Open Philanthropy) accounts for over half of philanthropic AI safety funding. SFF and Navigation Fund round out a top three that collectively dominates the space. New entrants like Astralis Foundation and the Frontier Model Forum's AI Safety Fund are diversifying the base, but slowly.

The most significant development of 2024–2025 was the **proliferation of government AI Safety Institutes** — the International Network now spans 11 members across three continents — and the passage of comprehensive AI legislation in the EU and South Korea. China's approach, combining aggressive sector-specific regulation with international engagement through CnAISDA, represents an increasingly sophisticated parallel track. Whether these parallel systems will converge toward shared safety standards or diverge into competing regulatory blocs remains the field's most consequential open question.
