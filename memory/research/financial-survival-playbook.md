## 2. 僑生 work regulations — what you legally can and cannot do

As a Malaysian Chinese student at NTU, you are classified as a **僑生** under the 僑生回國就學及輔導辦法. Your work rules are governed by **就業服務法 §50** and the **雇主聘僱外國人許可及管理辦法 §50–55**. The rules for 僑生 and 外籍生 are identical — collectively called 僑外生.

**The core rule: maximum 20 hours per week during semester, no weekly cap during winter/summer breaks** (but subject to 勞基法: 8 hrs/day, 40 hrs/week, 46 hrs overtime/month during breaks). You must hold a valid **work permit** from the Ministry of Labor, applied for through NTU's 僑生及陸生輔導組 via the online system at ezwp.wda.gov.tw. The permit costs just **NT$100**, is valid for up to **1 year** (extended from 6 months as of August 2024), and requires department advisor signature. Processing takes about 7 working days.

What counts as "work" is where things get nuanced. **Private tutoring unambiguously counts** — it constitutes providing labor services for pay and requires a work permit. **On-campus RA/TA positions also require a work permit** and count toward the 20-hour cap; NTU explicitly states this applies "regardless of on-campus or off-campus" work. Your current research stipend likely already counts.

**Freelancing on international platforms (Upwork, Fiverr) falls in a legal grey area.** The 就業服務法 focuses on employer-employee relationships, while freelancing is self-employment. However, the MOL's jurisdiction is based on **where work is physically performed**, not where the client is. Performing services while physically in Taiwan technically constitutes work in Taiwan, regardless of platform location. Similarly, **creating and selling online courses** is grey — the creation phase is "work" performed in Taiwan; passive revenue from already-created courses is more ambiguous. **Content creation income** (YouTube, blogging) follows the same logic.

**Receiving payment to a Malaysian bank account does not create a legal loophole** — the determining factor is where the labor is performed, not where payment arrives.

The penalties for violations are severe: **fines of NT$30,000–150,000** for working without a permit, potential **deportation plus a 3-year reentry ban**, and in severe cases a **lifetime work ban** in Taiwan. Employers face fines of NT$150,000–750,000. Forum discussions on Dcard suggest informal enforcement is lax for small-scale off-campus work, but detection via labor insurance records, tax withholding, and tip-offs does occur. One Dcard user, a former Malaysian 僑生, explicitly warned: *"被抓到就是罰款，甚至終身不能在台灣工作"*.

**Practical guidance:** Get a work permit (it's cheap and easy). For your RA work and any formal tutoring, stay within the 20-hour cap during semester. For international freelancing and course creation — these genuinely exist in a regulatory grey zone. The safest approach is to consult NTU's 僑生及陸生輔導組 directly. Note that digital product sales and passive income from previously created courses are the legally safest grey-area activities, since ongoing sales involve no continuous labor.

For taxes, as a resident (≥183 days/year in Taiwan), you face progressive rates starting at **5% on income up to NT$590,000** — most student income falls here. Foreign-source income under the 基本稅額條例 is exempt up to NT$7.5 million/year, but income from labor performed in Taiwan but paid from abroad may be classified as Taiwan-source income.

## 3. Scholarships you should apply for — ranked by value and stackability

The critical constraint is that many Taiwan government scholarships prohibit "double-dipping" with other government-funded stipends. If your current RA stipend comes from NSTC project funds, it likely counts as a government subsidy. **Focus on private foundations and international grants that explicitly allow stacking.**

**CTCI Foundation Research Scholarship for Overseas Students (中技社科技獎學金) — NT$150,000 one-time.** This is your single highest-value opportunity. Deadline is typically July–September annually. NTU is a designated university, each department can recommend one candidate, and the scholarship **explicitly allows stacking with other scholarships**. Your Interspeech paper gives you a strong edge. Only 15–30 recipients are selected across all universities. Apply through your department.

**Long-Term Future Fund (LTFF) Individual Grant — $5,000–200,000 USD.** As an AI Safety community leader running a university reading group, you are squarely in their target demographic. The LTFF funds AI safety research, community organizing, and career development. A grant application describing your community-building work in Taiwan — an underserved region for AI safety — could realistically secure **$5,000–20,000** (TWD 160,000–640,000). Applications are on a rolling/quarterly basis at funds.effectivealtruism.org/funds/far-future. This **does not conflict** with your RA stipend.

**Open Philanthropy / Kairos AI Safety University Group Organizer Funding** provides stipends and expenses specifically for graduate students organizing AI safety groups. As this is now managed through Kairos (formerly Open Phil directly), contact them about ongoing funding for your organizing work. This is essentially getting paid for something you already do.

**Interspeech 2026 ISCA Student Travel Grant — €500–2,500.** You have a first-author paper at Interspeech 2026 (Sydney, September 28 – October 1). Apply after paper acceptance is confirmed. Preference is given to participants from underrepresented regions.

**NSTC Conference Travel Grant (國科會補助研究生出席國際學術會議)** covers partial/full travel expenses to international conferences. Apply through NTU before the conference. Your Interspeech paper makes this nearly automatic.

**NTU Outstanding Overseas Chinese Graduate Student Scholarship (台大研究所優秀僑生獎學金) — NT$10,000/month.** Deadline is typically March each semester. However, it may conflict with your RA stipend if classified as a government subsidy — verify with 僑生及陸生輔導組 before applying.

**CTCI Foundation Bursary Award (中技社清寒助學金) — NT$100,000 one-time.** If you can demonstrate financial hardship (your declining savings qualify), this can be applied separately from the Research Scholarship. Same July–September deadline.

Additional opportunities worth pursuing: the **Jamie Lin Southeast Asia Scholarship** (NTU-specific, for Southeast Asian students), **OCAC Scholarship** (僑委會獎勵學行優良僑生獎學金, NT$6,000–20,000, September deadline — verify graduate student eligibility), **Manifund/Lightspeed Grants** for AI safety projects, and the **ARM Fund** for AI risk mitigation research.

If you later pursue a PhD, the landscape expands dramatically: Google PhD Fellowship, Microsoft Research Asia Fellowship ($10,000 USD), and Apple Scholars in AI/ML all match your research profile perfectly.
